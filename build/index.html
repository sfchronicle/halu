A night at Halu

Story and photography by Alison Christiana
June 20, 2017

Tucked away from the daytime bustle of Clement Street, Halu is inconspicuous, except for a small neon “open” sign that lights up in the early evening and beckons.

One step inside this neighborhood izakaya transports diners into a warm, whimsical world where 1960s and 1970s rock paraphernalia and brightly colored, handwritten menu specials occupy nearly every inch of the space. The Beatles’ “All You Need Is Love” dances over the buzz of intimate meetings, and savory aromas waft from the grill to whet your appetite.

Snapshots from the dining room at Halu. Erika and Sayaka, center, took over the restaurant when their father, Shig Komiyama, passed away several years ago.

Shig Komiyama and his wife, Mimi, opened Halu to share the yakitori recipes and izakaya-style dishes that they missed so dearly from Japan: everything from binchotan-grilled chicken thighs and gyoza to beef tongue and crunchy gizzards. For Shig, a masterful drummer within San Francisco’s rock scene, Halu was also a place where his community of artists, musicians and neighbors could gather over crisp beers and fare made with love.

Halu offers plenty of yakitori options. More than meat goes on the sticks; tomatoes, too.

I’d unknowingly passed Halu’s tiny doorway on my street many times, until a small sign piqued my interested. It read “no sushi today (or tomorrow).” I went inside and was instantly enamored with the food, the space and its delightful proprietors. I kept going back, and it wasn’t long until I was welcomed by name.

The tiny counter is often full at Halu.

In 2014, Shig passed away and the future of Halu hung in a tight balance. The restaurant closed for six months while the family grieved. His twin daughters, Erika and Sayaka, agreed that the best way to honor their father’s legacy was to reopen the doors of what had become their second home. For months, they stayed after hours, prepping for the next day’s service and learning to master the art of yakitori.

Beyond yakitori, Halu offers a variety of other dishes, including ramen and gyoza.

Having lost my own father years ago, I’m moved by this singular testament to a human who lived with such vibrancy and purpose. Halu was named after Shig’s mother, Haluko, and the Japanese word for spring.

For me, Halu also represents a place to celebrate the spirits of those whom we have loved.
